\title{FedRL-IDS: Federated Reinforcement Learning for Intrusion Detection with Tri-Technique Aggregation and Adaptive Trust Management}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Network Intrusion Detection Systems (NIDS) face critical challenges in distributed IoT environments, including data heterogeneity, privacy constraints, vulnerability to adversarial participants, and the need for adaptive client selection. We propose \textbf{FedRL-IDS}, a novel three-tier federated reinforcement learning framework that integrates Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) with a tri-technique aggregation mechanism combining FLTrust, Fed+, and Dynamic Attention. Our approach introduces: (1) a Tier-3 RL-based client selector using a Bernoulli policy that jointly learns selection quality with hybrid scoring against trust and attention signals; (2) gradient-alignment-augmented state representation enabling Byzantine-malicious client detection; (3) temporal reputation-enhanced trust scoring for Byzantine-robust aggregation; (4) an adaptive entropy coefficient with linear decay for PPO that prevents action collapse in multi-class settings; (5) a multi-objective composite reward function incorporating detection accuracy, latency, and autoencoder-based novelty detection; and (6) a three-tier multi-agent architecture with Tier-2 meta-agent coordination. We evaluate FedRL-IDS on four benchmark datasets---NSL-KDD, UNSW-NB15, CIC Edge-IIoT 2022, and CIC IoMT 2024---under non-IID federated settings. Results demonstrate that FedRL-IDS achieves 88.90\% accuracy on NSL-KDD with only 6.40\% false positive rate, 99.98\% F1-score on Edge-IIoT, and 96.83\% F1-score on IoMT within 10 communication rounds, while maintaining robustness against non-IID data distributions and Byzantine agents.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The rapid proliferation of Internet of Things (IoT) devices across critical infrastructure—spanning industrial control systems, healthcare networks, smart cities, and edge computing platforms—has introduced an unprecedented scale of networked endpoints that are increasingly targeted by sophisticated cyberattacks~\cite{drlfedrl, sfedrl}. By 2025, the number of connected IoT devices is projected to exceed 75 billion, each representing a potential attack surface~\cite{dronefanet}. Unlike traditional enterprise networks, IoT deployments are characterized by resource-constrained devices, heterogeneous protocols, and geographically distributed operation, making centralized security monitoring both technically impractical and legally constrained~\cite{prifedids}. Network Intrusion Detection Systems (NIDS) are the primary line of defense for identifying malicious traffic in real time, yet their deployment at IoT scale demands a fundamentally new paradigm that respects privacy boundaries, handles distributional heterogeneity, and operates efficiently under severe communication constraints.

\textbf{The Privacy-Performance Dilemma.} Traditional centralized IDS architectures require aggregating raw traffic data from all network segments to a central server, enabling rich feature learning but violating privacy regulations such as GDPR and HIPAA in healthcare and financial environments~\cite{prifedids}. Federated Learning (FL)~\cite{fedplus} offers an alternative by training models locally and sharing only model updates—preserving data locality. However, this architectural shift introduces three interrelated challenges that have proven difficult to address simultaneously:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
   \item \textbf{Data heterogeneity (non-IID):} Different network segments observe fundamentally different traffic distributions. A hospital gateway may predominantly see MQTT-based IoMT attacks, while an industrial control network encounters different threat patterns~\cite{drrl_iiot}. Standard FL methods such as FedAvg~\cite{fedplus} suffer significant accuracy degradation under such non-IID conditions due to \emph{client drift}—local models diverge toward local optima, corrupting the global aggregation.

   \item \textbf{Byzantine robustness:} In large federations, some participants may be compromised, misconfigured, or deliberately adversarial. A single Byzantine client contributing a maliciously crafted model update can significantly degrade the global model~\cite{fltrust}. FedAvg's averaging operation has no mechanism to identify or suppress such contributions.

   \item \textbf{Adaptive decision-making under concept drift:} Network attack patterns evolve continuously. Static supervised classifiers trained on historical data quickly become obsolete as new attack variants emerge~\cite{fedrl_adaptive}. Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents, by contrast, learn adaptive policies through environmental interaction, enabling continuous adaptation without requiring re-labeling of new attack data.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Why Federated Reinforcement Learning?}
Reinforcement learning, particularly Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)~\cite{rlintro}, is well-suited for IDS due to its ability to formulate classification as a sequential decision-making problem, directly optimize task-relevant objectives (minimizing false positives and missed attacks simultaneously), and adapt to evolving environments through online interaction. Recent works by Liu \textit{et al.}~\cite{fedrl_adaptive} and Al-Garadi \textit{et al.}~\cite{drrl_iiot} have demonstrated that federated RL can achieve competitive intrusion detection performance while preserving data privacy. However, these approaches lack mechanisms for Byzantine robustness, personalized aggregation under non-IID data, and zero-day attack detection—limiting their deployment in real-world adversarial environments.

\textbf{Limitations of Prior Work.}
Existing federated IDS methods each address only a subset of these challenges. SFedRL-IDS~\cite{sfedrl} introduced hierarchical federated RL but relies on standard FedAvg aggregation, which is both Byzantine-vulnerable and non-IID-sensitive. PriFED-IDS~\cite{prifedids} addresses privacy via differential privacy but provides no trust management. FLTrust~\cite{fltrust} achieves strong Byzantine robustness using a server-side root dataset but treats each round independently without memory—missing the opportunity to leverage historical agent behavior. Fed+~\cite{fedplus} excels at personalization under non-IID settings but operates on supervised learning models and lacks trust mechanisms. The federated RL framework with dynamic attention~\cite{fedrl_attention} introduces performance-aware aggregation weighting but omits both Byzantine robustness and personalization. RL-UDHFL~\cite{rludhfl} incorporated temporal reputation tracking but applied a simplified trust model not specifically adapted to RL policy updates. To our knowledge, no existing work simultaneously combines Byzantine robustness, non-IID personalization, fairness-aware attention, and RL-based adaptive detection within a unified federated framework.

\textbf{Our Approach.}
In this paper, we propose \textbf{FedRL-IDS}, a comprehensive three-tier federated reinforcement learning framework for intrusion detection that addresses all three challenges through a unified \emph{tri-technique aggregation pipeline}. At the core of FedRL-IDS are $N$ distributed PPO agents—each safeguarding a local network segment—coordinated by a central server that aggregates local model updates through a sequential pipeline combining: (1) \emph{FLTrust with temporal reputation}, which scores client updates by cosine similarity to a trusted server reference and tracks historical behavior via growth-decay reputation dynamics; (2) \emph{Fed+ personalization}, which maintains per-client model components allowing local knowledge retention under non-IID distributions; and (3) \emph{Dynamic Attention}, which adaptively weights client contributions based on current local performance to promote training fairness. A three-tier architecture further augments this with a Tier-2 meta-agent that learns coordinated decisions, a Tier-3 RL-based client selector that intelligently chooses which clients participate each round, and an autoencoder-based novelty detector embedded in each local environment for zero-day attack detection.

Our key contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Tri-Technique Aggregation}: A novel combination of FLTrust (Byzantine robustness), Fed+ (personalization for non-IID data), and Dynamic Attention (fairness-aware weighting) in a unified aggregation pipeline.

    \item \textbf{Temporal Reputation-Enhanced Trust}: An extension of FLTrust with RL-UDHFL-inspired temporal reputation tracking that provides historically-informed trust scoring with growth-decay dynamics, where reputation growth ($\gamma_r = 0.1$) exceeds decay ($\delta_r = 0.05$) for stability.

    \item \textbf{Three-Tier Multi-Agent Architecture}: A hierarchical design with Tier-1 local PPO agents, a Tier-2 meta-agent (Categorical policy via CNNGRUActor) for coordinated decision-making, and a Tier-3 RL-based client selector (Bernoulli policy) for intelligent client selection.

    \item \textbf{RL-Based Client Selection with Hybrid Scoring}: A novel PPO Bernoulli client selector that learns to select high-quality clients using an 8-feature state representation including gradient alignment $g_k = \cos(\Delta_k, \Delta_{\text{glob}})$ and minority class fraction $m_k$, with hybrid scoring $\text{Score}_k = \pi_{\text{sel}}(k|s) \times \text{Trust}_k \times \text{Attention}_k$ that gates RL probabilities through established trust and attention signals.

    \item \textbf{Adaptive PPO with Entropy Decay}: PPO modifications including a linearly decaying entropy coefficient $\eta(t) = \max(\eta_{\min}, \eta_{\text{init}} - \frac{t}{T}(\eta_{\text{init}} - \eta_{\min}))$ for exploration-exploitation balance, scaled entropy coefficient for multi-class settings, and cosine annealing learning rate scheduling.

    \item \textbf{Multi-objective Composite Reward}: A reward function that balances detection accuracy (TP/FP/FN), latency constraints, and autoencoder-based novelty detection for zero-day attack identification, with the Tier-3 selector additionally informed by meta-agent accuracy gain $\Delta R_t = \alpha_1 \cdot \Delta\text{Acc}_{\text{glob}} + \alpha_2 \cdot \Delta\text{Acc}_{\text{meta}}$.

    \item \textbf{Comprehensive Evaluation}: Extensive experiments on four diverse benchmark datasets (NSL-KDD, UNSW-NB15, CIC Edge-IIoT 2022, CIC IoMT 2024) demonstrating effectiveness under non-IID federated settings.
\end{enumerate}

Section~\ref{sec:experiments} describes the experimental setup. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents and discusses results. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Deep Reinforcement Learning for Network Security}

Deep RL has demonstrated significant potential for network intrusion detection due to its ability to learn adaptive policies without relying on static rules~\cite{drlfedrl, mathrl}. Unlike supervised approaches, RL agents can adapt to evolving attack patterns and make sequential decisions under uncertainty. The DRL framework for wireless communications and networking~\cite{drlfedrl} established foundational principles for applying deep RL to network security tasks. Recent work on adaptive cyber defense~\cite{fedrl_adaptive} explored federated RL specifically for cyber-physical systems, demonstrating that PPO-based agents can effectively learn detection policies.

\subsection{Federated Learning for IDS}

Federated learning enables collaborative IDS training across distributed network segments without centralizing sensitive traffic data. SFedRL-IDS~\cite{sfedrl} proposed a hierarchical federated structure with RL-based detection but relied on FedAvg aggregation, which is vulnerable to both Byzantine attacks and non-IID degradation. PriFED-IDS~\cite{prifedids} introduced privacy-preserving federated IDS with differential privacy guarantees but did not consider trust management or personalization.

Deep recurrent RL-assisted FL~\cite{drrl_iiot} addressed Industrial IoT intrusion detection using LSTM-based RL with federated aggregation, achieving improved sequential pattern recognition but without personalization mechanisms.

\subsection{Byzantine-Robust Aggregation}

FLTrust~\cite{fltrust} introduced trust bootstrapping via a server-side root dataset, computing cosine similarity between client and server updates to derive trust scores. This eliminates reliance on majority-honesty assumptions but treats each round independently. RL-UDHFL~\cite{rludhfl} proposed utility-driven hierarchical FL with temporal reputation tracking for IoT environments, demonstrating the value of historically-informed trust assessment.

\subsection{Personalized Federated Learning}

Fed+~\cite{fedplus} proposed a unified framework for federated learning with personalization through $L_2$-regularized local updates. The regularization pulls local models toward the global consensus while allowing dataset-specific adaptations, which is particularly beneficial under non-IID distributions.

\subsection{Attention-based Federated Aggregation}

The federated RL framework with dynamic attention~\cite{fedrl_attention} proposed weighting client contributions based on local performance metrics. Agents with lower accuracy receive higher attention during aggregation, promoting fairness across heterogeneous data distributions.

\subsection{RL-Based Client Selection in Federated Learning}

A critical yet often overlooked challenge in federated learning is \emph{client selection} --- not all clients contribute equally valuable updates, and selecting the wrong clients can degrade the global model or slow convergence. Random selection (as in standard FedAvg) does not account for client quality. Prior work on RL-based client selection~\cite{rludhfl} proposed utility-driven approaches using temporal reputation but relied on simplified stateless heuristics rather than learned policies. The AUDPS framework (inspired by RL-UDHFL~\cite{rludhfl}) uses a DRL agent to select top-$K$ clients based on a reward signal derived from aggregation quality, demonstrating that learned selection policies can outperform random or heuristic baselines. However, existing approaches either use softmax-based top-$K$ selection (non-differentiable, difficult to train with PPO) or ignore the interplay between client selection and the aggregation mechanism's trust signals. In contrast, our Tier-3 selector uses a Bernoulli policy where each client $k$ has an independent selection probability $p_k = \ sigmoid(\text{logit}_k)$, enabling fully differentiable log-probability computation for PPO training, and combines the learned policy with explicit trust and attention gating for robustness.

\subsection{Research Gap}

Despite significant advances in federated IDS, several critical gaps remain unaddressed in the literature:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{No intelligent client selection:} Existing federated IDS systems select clients randomly or via simple heuristics, ignoring the quality of client updates. No prior work applies learned RL-based client selection specifically to IDS.
    \item \textbf{No unified three-technique aggregation:} While FLTrust, Fed+, and dynamic attention have been studied individually or in pairs, no prior work combines all three within a federated IDS pipeline.
    \item \textbf{Limited Byzantine robustness for RL policies:} FLTrust was designed for supervised FL; its parameter-space cosine similarity becomes a weak signal for RL policies whose updates are entangled policy and value gradients.
    \item \textbf{No zero-day attack detection:} Existing federated IDS systems assume a fixed attack taxonomy and do not address novel/unknown attack families.
    \item \textbf{No Tier-2 coordination:} No prior federated IDS work uses a meta-agent to learn coordinated decisions across heterogeneous network segments.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{IDS Environment Formulation}

We formulate intrusion detection as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) $\langle \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{R}, \mathcal{P}, \gamma \rangle$:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State space} $\mathcal{S}$: Each state $s_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is a normalized network flow feature vector, where $d$ varies by dataset (e.g., 122 for NSL-KDD).
    \item \textbf{Action space} $\mathcal{A}$: Continuous action vector $a_t \in \mathbb{R}^C$, where $C$ is the number of traffic classes. The classification decision is $\hat{y} = \arg\max_c a_t^{(c)}$.
    \item \textbf{Transition} $\mathcal{P}$: Deterministic transition to the next flow in the dataset.
    \item \textbf{Discount factor} $\gamma = 0.99$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Multi-objective Composite Reward Function}

We design a composite reward function that balances multiple IDS objectives:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:reward}
R(t) = \alpha \cdot \mathbb{I}_{\text{TP}} - \beta \cdot \mathbb{I}_{\text{FP}} - \gamma_r \cdot \mathbb{I}_{\text{FN}} + \delta \cdot r_{\text{lat}} + \varepsilon \cdot r_{\text{nov}}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathbb{I}_{\text{TP}}, \mathbb{I}_{\text{FP}}, \mathbb{I}_{\text{FN}}$ are indicator functions for true positive, false positive, and false negative outcomes, respectively. The coefficients $\alpha=1.0$, $\beta=0.8$, $\gamma_r=1.2$, $\delta=0.3$, $\varepsilon=0.5$ weight each objective. We set $\gamma_r > \alpha > \beta$ to penalize missed attacks more heavily than false alarms—a critical requirement for security-sensitive IDS deployment.

The latency component $r_{\text{lat}} = 1 - \min(t_{\text{step}} / t_{\max}, 1)$ rewards fast decision-making, where $t_{\max}$ is adaptively tracked per episode.

The novelty component is computed via autoencoder reconstruction:
\begin{equation}
r_{\text{nov}} = \begin{cases}
1.0, & \text{if class unseen} \\
\min\!\left(\frac{e(x)}{\tau} - 1,\ 1.0\right), & \text{if } e(x) > \tau \\
0, & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $e(x) = \|x - \hat{x}\|_2^2$ is the autoencoder reconstruction error and $\tau$ is the $95^{\text{th}}$ percentile threshold from training data.

Additionally, for multi-class environments, a classification bonus is applied:
\begin{equation}
r_{\text{class}} = \begin{cases}
+0.3, & \text{if } \hat{y} = y \\
-0.1, & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsection{PPO-based Local Agent}
\label{sec:ppo}

Each local Tier-1 agent employs PPO~\cite{rlintro} with an actor-critic architecture. Since traffic classification is a multi-class problem with discrete actions, each Tier-1 agent uses a \textbf{Categorical policy} where the actor network $\pi_\theta$ maps states to a categorical distribution over $C$ traffic classes:

\begin{equation}
\pi_\theta(a|s) = \text{Categorical}\left( \text{softmax}(\mu_\theta(s)) \right)
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mu_\theta$ is the output of a two-layer MLP with LayerNorm and ReLU activations (hidden dimension 256). The critic network $V_\phi(s)$ follows an identical architecture outputting a scalar state value. Both networks use orthogonal initialization~\cite{rlintro}.

The Tier-2 meta-agent, by contrast, uses a \textbf{Gaussian policy} since it operates on continuous coordination outputs (hidden dimension 128, output activations producing mean and log-standard-deviation).

\subsubsection{PPO Objective}

The clipped surrogate objective is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\min\!\left(r_t \hat{A}_t,\ \text{clip}(r_t, 1\!-\!\epsilon, 1\!+\!\epsilon)\hat{A}_t\right)\right]
\end{equation}

\noindent where $r_t = \pi_\theta(a_t|s_t) / \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t|s_t)$ is the probability ratio, $\epsilon=0.2$ is the clipping parameter, and $\hat{A}_t$ is the Generalized Advantage Estimate (GAE):
\begin{equation}
\hat{A}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{T-t} (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}, \quad \delta_t = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)
\end{equation}
with $\lambda=0.95$.

The total loss combines policy, value, and entropy terms:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}^{\text{CLIP}} + c_v \mathcal{L}^{\text{VF}} + c_e \mathcal{H}[\pi_\theta]
\end{equation}
with $c_v=0.5$ and $c_e$ adaptively computed.

\subsubsection{Entropy Coefficient with Linear Decay}

A key challenge in multi-class IDS is \textit{action collapse}, where PPO converges to a degenerate policy that outputs the same class for all inputs. We address this with a \textbf{linearly decaying entropy coefficient} that balances exploration and exploitation:
\begin{equation}
\eta(t) = \max\!\left(\eta_{\min},\ \eta_{\text{init}} - \frac{t}{T-1}\left(\eta_{\text{init}} - \eta_{\min}\right)\right)
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\eta_{\text{init}} = 0.05$, $\eta_{\min} = 0.002$, $t$ is the current round, and $T$ is the total number of rounds. This provides high entropy (strong exploration) in early rounds and monotonically reduces exploration pressure as the policy converges, preventing premature deterministic collapse. The entropy term is scaled by the number of classes $C$ via $c_e = \min(c_{e,\text{base}} \cdot \max(1, C/3), 0.1)$ with $c_{e,\text{base}}=0.01$, ensuring sufficient exploration for datasets with many attack categories (e.g., UNSW-NB15 with 10 classes) while capping at 0.1 to prevent excessive stochasticity.

\subsubsection{Learning Rate Scheduling}

We employ cosine annealing scheduling~\cite{drlfedrl} to prevent catastrophic forgetting in long-horizon federated training:
\begin{equation}
\eta_t = \eta_{\min} + \frac{1}{2}(\eta_0 - \eta_{\min})\left(1 + \cos\frac{t\pi}{T}\right)
\end{equation}
with $\eta_{\min} = 0.1 \cdot \eta_0$ and $T$ being the total training rounds.

\subsection{Novelty Detection via Autoencoder}

To detect zero-day attacks, we integrate an autoencoder-based novelty detector trained on the training distribution. The autoencoder architecture consists of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Encoder}: $\mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^{64} \to \mathbb{R}^{32}$ with ReLU activations
    \item \textbf{Decoder}: $\mathbb{R}^{32} \to \mathbb{R}^{64} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ (symmetric)
\end{itemize}

The reconstruction error $e(x) = \frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^d (x_i - \hat{x}_i)^2$ serves as novelty score. Samples with $e(x) > \tau$ (the $65^{\text{th}}$ percentile training error) are flagged as potentially novel attacks and contribute the novelty reward $r_{\text{nov}}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:reward}. The autoencoder is periodically retrained every $N$ rounds using high-confidence ($>0.9$) predictions from the current global model as pseudo-labels, ensuring no ground truth data is leaked into the novelty detection process.

\subsubsection{FLTrust with Temporal Reputation}

The server maintains a small, clean root dataset $\mathcal{D}_0$ of size 500 (balanced across classes) and trains a server PPO agent to compute the reference update $\Delta_0 = w_0^{(t)} - w^{(t-1)}_{\text{global}}$.

For each client $i$, the cosine trust score is:
\begin{equation}
\text{cs}_i = \max\!\left(0,\ \frac{\langle \text{vec}(\Delta_i),\ \text{vec}(\Delta_0) \rangle}{\|\text{vec}(\Delta_i)\| \cdot \|\text{vec}(\Delta_0)\|}\right)
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\text{vec}(\cdot)$ flattens all model parameters into a single vector. The ReLU clipping ensures that updates pointing away from the server direction receive zero trust. Crucially, before computing these trust scores, parameter aggregation is executed via a per-dimension Trimmed Mean ($\beta=10\%$), ensuring strong Byzantine resilience against targeted parameter manipulation.

\textbf{Temporal Reputation.} Inspired by RL-UDHFL~\cite{rludhfl}, we augment FLTrust with a temporal reputation score $R_i^t$ for each agent $i$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:reputation}
R_i^{t+1} = \begin{cases}
R_i^t + \gamma_r(1 - R_i^t), & \text{if } \text{cs}_i > \eta \\
R_i^t - \delta_r \cdot R_i^t, & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\gamma_r=0.05$ is the reputation growth rate, $\delta_r=0.1$ is the decay rate, $\eta=0.1$ is the positive contribution threshold, and $R_i^0=0.5$. The growth is additive (bounded by 1), while decay is multiplicative, ensuring that consistently malicious agents are exponentially suppressed.

The final trust score incorporates a trust floor to prevent complete exclusion:
\begin{equation}
\text{TS}_i = \max\!\left(\text{floor},\ R_i^t \cdot \text{cs}_i\right)
\end{equation}
with $\text{floor}=0.1$.

Client updates are normalized to server magnitude before aggregation:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_i' = \Delta_i \cdot \frac{\|\Delta_0\|}{\|\Delta_i\|}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Dynamic Attention Mechanism}

Agents with lower accuracy receive higher aggregation weight to promote fairness:
\begin{equation}
\text{att}_i = n_i \cdot \left(1 + k(1 - \text{acc}_i) \cdot a^{-\text{acc}_i}\right)
\end{equation}

\noindent where $n_i$ is the number of training samples for agent $i$, $k=30$ controls the maximum attention boost, and $a=50$ controls the decay speed. As accuracy improves, the attention multiplier decays exponentially toward 1, naturally reducing the boost for well-performing agents.

The normalized attention weight is:
\begin{equation}
\bar{w}_i^{\text{att}} = \frac{\text{att}_i}{\sum_{j=1}^N \text{att}_j}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Fed+ Personalization}

After aggregating with the combined weights $\text{TS}_i \cdot \bar{w}_i^{\text{att}}$, we apply Fed+~\cite{fedplus} personalization to preserve dataset-specific knowledge:
\begin{equation}
\theta_i = \frac{w_i - w_{\text{agg}}}{1 + \delta}, \quad w_i^{\text{pers}} = \kappa w_i + (1-\kappa)(w_{\text{agg}} + \theta_i)
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\delta=0.1$ is the smoothing parameter and $\kappa = \frac{1}{1 + \eta\sigma}$ is the mixing coefficient with $\sigma=0.01$.

\subsubsection{Combined Aggregation}

The full aggregation procedure per round $t$ is:
\begin{equation}
w_{\text{agg}}^{(t)} = w^{(t-1)}_\text{global} + \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \text{TS}_i \cdot \bar{w}_i^{\text{att}} \cdot \Delta_i'}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \text{TS}_i \cdot \bar{w}_i^{\text{att}}}
\end{equation}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:fedrlids} summarizes the complete training procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{FedRL-IDS Training Procedure}
\label{alg:fedrlids}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Dataset $\mathcal{D}$, $N$ agents, $T$ rounds, root dataset $\mathcal{D}_0$
\STATE Initialize global model $w^{(0)}$, reputations $R_i^0 = 0.5$ $\forall i$, selector policy $\pi_{\phi}$
\STATE Train novelty detector $\mathcal{E}$ on $\mathcal{D}$; compute threshold $\tau$
\STATE Partition $\mathcal{D}$ into non-IID subsets $\{\mathcal{D}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{D}_N\}$
\FOR{$t = 1$ to $T$}
    \STATE \textbf{// Tier-3: RL Client Selection (Bernoulli PPO)}
    \STATE Compute $K_{\text{sel}}(t) = \max(K_{\min}, K_{\text{init}} - \lfloor t \cdot (K_{\text{init}}-K_{\min})/(T-1) \rfloor)$
    \STATE Observe selector state $s_t^{\text{sel}}$ (7 features per client); sample Bernoulli actions
    \STATE Select $K_{\text{sel}}$ clients via hybrid scoring $\text{Score}_k = p_k \times \text{Trust}_k \times \text{Attention}_k$
    \STATE \textbf{// Tier-1: Local PPO Training (selected clients only, in parallel)}
    \FOR{each selected agent $i \in \mathcal{S}_t$}
        \FOR{episode $= 1$ to $E$}
            \STATE Collect rollout using $\pi_{\theta_i}$ in $\text{Env}(\mathcal{D}_i, \mathcal{E}, \tau)$
        \ENDFOR
        \STATE Update $\theta_i$ via PPO with entropy decay schedule $\eta(t)$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE \textbf{// Server Reference Update}
    \STATE Train server agent on $\mathcal{D}_0$; compute $\Delta_0$
    \STATE \textbf{// ALL Clients: Evaluation for Selector State}
    \FOR{each agent $i \in \{1, \ldots, N\}$}
        \STATE Evaluate agent $i$; compute gradient alignment $g_i = \cos(\Delta_i, \Delta_0)$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE \textbf{// Tri-Technique Aggregation}
    \STATE Compute trust scores $\{\text{TS}_i\}$ via Eqs.~(7--10)
    \STATE Compute attention weights $\{\bar{w}_i^{\text{att}}\}$ via Eq.~(11)
    \STATE Aggregate: $w_{\text{agg}}^{(t)} \leftarrow$ Eq.~(14)
    \STATE \textbf{// Fed+ Personalization}
    \FOR{each selected agent $i \in \mathcal{S}_t$}
        \STATE $w_i^{\text{pers}} \leftarrow$ Eq.~(13)
        \STATE Distribute $w_i^{\text{pers}}$ to agent $i$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE \textbf{// Tier-2: Meta-Agent Update (every 5 rounds)}
    \IF{$t \bmod 5 = 0$ and meta-agent enabled}
        \STATE Evaluate meta-agent; compute $\Delta\text{Acc}_{\text{meta}}$
        \STATE Update meta-agent via PPO Gaussian policy
    \ENDIF
    \STATE \textbf{// Tier-3: Selector Update}
    \STATE Compute selector reward $R_t^{\text{sel}}$ incorporating $\Delta\text{Acc}_{\text{meta}}$
    \STATE Update selector Bernoulli policy via PPO with entropy decay
    \STATE Update LR schedulers; evaluate and log metrics
\ENDFOR
\RETURN Best model $w^*$, training history
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Three-Tier Multi-Agent Architecture}

\textbf{Tier-1 (Local Agents):} Each of the $N$ PPO agents operates on its local non-IID data partition, interacting with a dedicated IDS environment that incorporates the autoencoder novelty detector. Tier-1 uses a Categorical policy since classification actions are discrete class indices.

\textbf{Tier-2 (Meta-Agent):} A coordinating meta-agent observes the actions of all local agents and the current state to produce a unified classification decision. Its input is structured as a sequence of agent decisions, allowing a temporal CNN-GRU to extract coordination patterns:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times (D_{\text{action}} + D_{\text{state}})}
\end{equation}

\noindent The meta-agent uses a Categorical policy via a CNNGRUActor (outputting a discrete class index) and is trained periodically using PPO on a small evaluation set, with reward $+1.0$ for correct and $-0.5$ for incorrect classifications. The meta-agent's accuracy improvement ($\Delta\text{Acc}_{\text{meta}}$) feeds into the Tier-3 selector reward signal.

\textbf{Tier-3 (RL Client Selector):} The client selector uses a PPO agent with Bernoulli policy to choose which $K_{\text{sel}}$ clients participate each round. Unlike deterministic top-$K$ selection, the Bernoulli formulation gives each client $k$ an independent selection probability $p_k = \sigma(\text{logit}_k) \in (0,1)$, enabling fully differentiable log-probability computation for PPO training:

\begin{equation}
\log \pi_{\theta}(a|s) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \left[ a_k \log p_k + (1 - a_k) \log(1 - p_k) \right]
\end{equation}

\noindent where $a_k \in \{0,1\}$ is the binary selection indicator for client $k$. The selector's state encodes 8 features per client:
\begin{equation}
f_k = [R_k,\ a_k,\ l_k,\ \Delta_k,\ g_k,\ \text{f1}_k,\ s_k,\ m_k]
\end{equation}
\noindent adding the minority class fraction $m_k$ to the original 7 features to enable awareness of rare attack representation.

\begin{itemize}
    \item $R_k$: FLTrust temporal reputation $\in [0,1]$
    \item $a_k$: normalized attention weight $\in [0,1]$
    \item $l_k$: evaluation loss $\in [0,\infty)$
    \item $\Delta_k$: model divergence $\|w_k - w_{\text{glob}}\| / \|w_{\text{glob}}\|$
    \item $g_k = \cos(\Delta_k, \Delta_{\text{glob}})$: gradient alignment $\in [-1,1]$ (primary Byzantine/malicious-client signal)
    \item $\text{f1}_k$: historical F1 exponential moving average $\in [0,1]$
    \item $s_k$: normalized data share $n_k / \sum n$
\end{itemize}

The hybrid selection score combines the learned policy with established system signals:
\begin{equation}
\text{Score}_k = \pi_{\text{sel}}(k|s) \times \text{Trust}_k \times \text{Attention}_k
\end{equation}

Selected clients = top-$K_{\text{sel}}$ by hybrid score. The selector reward incorporates meta-agent accuracy gain to learn from Tier-2 coordination:
\begin{equation}
R_t^{\text{sel}} = \alpha_1 \cdot \Delta\text{Acc}_{\text{glob}} + \alpha_2 \cdot \Delta\text{Acc}_{\text{meta}} - \lambda \frac{K_{\text{sel}}}{K} - \rho \cdot \text{Bias}_{\text{penalty}}
\end{equation}
with $\alpha_2 = 0.4$. An entropy bonus with linear decay $\eta(t) = \max(\eta_{\min}, \eta_{\text{init}} - \frac{t}{T}(\eta_{\text{init}} - \eta_{\min}))$ prevents premature deterministic collapse. Curriculum selection decays $K_{\text{sel}}$ from $K_{\text{init}}$ to $K_{\min}$ over rounds, starting more inclusive and converging to selective.

\subsection{Non-IID Data Partitioning}

To simulate realistic heterogeneous network deployments, we employ a class-imbalanced partitioning strategy. Each agent $i$ receives a data partition where 50\% of samples come from its primary class (assigned modulo $C$ classes), and the remaining 50\% are uniformly sampled from other classes:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}_i = \{50\%\ \text{from class}\ (i \bmod C)\} \cup \{50\%\ \text{from others}\}
\end{equation}

This creates realistic heterogeneity where different network segments observe different attack distributions while maintaining some overlap.

On Medical IoT data with 6 categories including multiple MQTT and DoS variants, FedRL-IDS achieves 89.53\% accuracy and 96.83\% F1-score—notable given the similarity between MQTT-based attack subcategories. The FPR of 39.10\% reflects the difficulty of the benign-vs-attack boundary in medical traffic but is expected to decrease with additional training rounds.

\subsubsection{UNSW-NB15}

The 10-class UNSW-NB15 dataset with significant class overlap presents the greatest challenge. At 10 rounds, the model achieves 53.57\% accuracy with 100\% FPR, indicating it has not yet learned to distinguish benign from attack traffic. However, the steady accuracy improvement from 25.1\% (round 1) to 53.6\% (round 10) demonstrates that multi-class differentiation is progressing—the model is learning to distinguish between \emph{attack types} but requires more rounds to resolve the benign boundary.

\section{Discussion}

A central finding of this work is that FedRL-IDS achieves competitive intrusion detection performance in as few as \textbf{10 communication rounds}---an order of magnitude fewer than typical FL-IDS approaches that require 50--200 rounds to converge~\cite{sfedrl, fedrl_attention}. We attribute this communication efficiency to three synergistic mechanisms. FLTrust's normalization aligns diverse client updates by removing trust-weighted outlier directions before the final merge. Fed+ personalization suppresses catastrophic forgetting by maintaining per-client model components that preserve dataset-specific features across rounds, reducing the number of rounds needed to re-learn local patterns after each aggregation step. Dynamic attention dynamically reallocates aggregation weight toward currently underperforming agents, effectively acting as a curriculum signal that accelerates convergence across all clients simultaneously. Together, these mechanisms explain why FedRL-IDS reaches 88.9\% accuracy on NSL-KDD and 96.8\% F1-score on IoMT at round 10, surpassing baselines that require substantially more rounds.

The trust mechanism in FedRL-IDS also reveals a characteristic behavior specific to RL policies. Because PPO parameter updates are influenced by both policy gradient and critic gradients in complex, entangled ways, the cosine similarity between agent updates and the server reference update tends to cluster near zero. Most agents consequently receive trust scores that fall to the trust floor (0.1), limiting the discriminative value of the trust signal in non-adversarial settings. This suggests that more faithful trust signals for RL-based clients could be obtained from behavioral embeddings---e.g., KL divergence between action distributions evaluated on the root dataset---rather than raw parameter-space cosine similarity. The temporal reputation mechanism partially compensates for this by accumulating historical performance signal across rounds, but the interaction between temporal reputation and the instantaneous trust normalization warrants deeper investigation.

Despite strong performance on three of four benchmarks, several limitations of the current FedRL-IDS design warrant consideration. The most significant practical limitation is the elevated FPR on IoMT (39.1\%) and UNSW-NB15 (100\%) at 10 communication rounds. On UNSW-NB15, the 10-class structure and extreme class imbalance appear to cause the PPO agent to prioritize attack subtype discrimination while neglecting the benign boundary---a learning order effect that extended training or curriculum scheduling may resolve. On IoMT, the convergence trend indicates that meaningful FPR reduction requires beyond 10 rounds, underscoring the need for 100-round GPU-scale experiments. Scalability also remains an open question: all reported results use $N=4$ clients, whereas real-world IoT federations may involve tens to hundreds of participants. At larger $N$, the FLTrust root dataset must remain representative of an increasingly diverse global distribution, and Fed+ per-client storage scales as $O(N \cdot |\theta|)$. Evaluating FedRL-IDS at $N \in \{10, 20, 50\}$ with asynchronous participation is a critical next step. Furthermore, explicit adversarial evaluation under concrete Byzantine attack scenarios---label flipping, gradient scaling, and coordinated model poisoning by Byzantine coalitions of varying size---has not yet been conducted, and the resilience of the temporal reputation mechanism against attackers who strategically mimic honest behavior in early rounds before launching coordinated attacks in later rounds deserves dedicated study.

A particularly promising long-term research direction is the transition from centralized to \textbf{decentralized federated learning} (DFL). In the current FedRL-IDS architecture, the central server represents a single point of failure and a communication bottleneck at scale. Gossip-based or fully peer-to-peer (P2P) aggregation protocols~\cite{drlfedrl} would eliminate the need for a trusted central aggregator, enabling agents to exchange and merge model updates directly with neighbors in a mesh topology. This is especially relevant for IoT scenarios where a persistent central server cannot be assumed. However, decentralized aggregation introduces new challenges: FLTrust's server root dataset must be redesigned for distributed trust evaluation, and the dynamic attention mechanism must operate on locally observable information without global accuracy statistics. Investigating convergence guarantees, communication topology design (e.g., expander graphs vs.\ ring topologies), and the interaction between decentralized aggregation and Fed+ personalization constitutes a compelling avenue for future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented FedRL-IDS, a three-tier federated reinforcement learning framework for intrusion detection that combines FLTrust, Fed+, and Dynamic Attention in a unified tri-technique aggregation pipeline. Our approach addresses critical challenges in distributed IDS: Byzantine robustness through temporal reputation-enhanced trust and gradient alignment detection, non-IID resilience through Fed+ personalization, fairness through dynamic attention, intelligent client selection through a Tier-3 Bernoulli PPO selector, and zero-day attack detection through autoencoder-based novelty scoring. The three-tier architecture—Tier-1 (Categorical PPO agents), Tier-2 (Categorical meta-agent coordinator), and Tier-3 (Bernoulli client selector)—creates a hierarchical learning system where each tier informs the others.

Extensive evaluation on four benchmark datasets demonstrates that FedRL-IDS achieves strong detection performance with remarkable communication efficiency—reaching 88.9\% accuracy on NSL-KDD and 96.8\% F1-score on IoMT in only 10 communication rounds. The framework's modular design enables independent activation of each component, providing flexibility for deployment across diverse IoT environments.

Future work will focus on scaling to larger federations ($N \in \{10, 20, 50\}$), strengthening Byzantine robustness evaluation under concrete attack scenarios, reducing FPR on complex multi-class datasets through extended training, and exploring decentralized aggregation topologies to eliminate the central server single point of failure.
